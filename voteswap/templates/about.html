After the election

After the election, while the surprisingly high third-party turnout is still on everyone's minds, we can start a dialogue on changing our election process to use ranked choice voting, aka instant runoff elections.

Ranked choice voting

This system of voting lets you write down your candidates in order, so if your first choice doesn't win, your vote goes to your second choice, and so on. This allows votes for third parties (or fourth, or fifth, or ...) to not be wasted and let the least favored candidate win.

Ranked choice voting is used around the world: cities and states in the United States, congressional elections in Australia, and for various elections in India, New Zealand, Ireland, and the United Kingdom.

Ranked choice voting is not without its flaws, but they can be addressed by using the Condorcet method to count votes. Condorcet voting prevents a few counter-intuitive outcomes of ranked choice voting, and should be our preferred method.
